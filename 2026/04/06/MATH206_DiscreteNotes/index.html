Sample Space

In the book, the probability theory is called a measure of uncertainty. This is a good description. When I learned this chapter, I thought of the probability theory as a simulation of real-world cases in math models. People try to explain the happening and results of specific cases in set and wants to analyze them by function.

Consider this case, a person throw a 6-sides dice with number 1 to 6 and we want to know what’s the number on the top. The sample space of this case can simply be the set,

We can’t get more information actually, because we don’t know this person and his/her dice at all. What if the person is bad who has a designed dice which is more likely to result in 6 at the top? We can’t assume the result is in uniform distribution unless we examinate the dice carefully.

Throw a coin, count the number of trails until we get head. Then the sample space is $\mathbb{N}$

Pick a random point in the x-axis, the sample space would be $\mathbb{R}$

You see.. the sample space can be finite or infinite, continuous or discrete.

When things are easy

People use $P(A)$, to see the probability (in the range $[0,1]$) of certain event $A$. By the way, an event is a subset of the sample space.

It is very natural to see $P(S)$, let $S$ being the set of whole sample space, $P(S)=1$. It means, in the case, every result is measured as a element in sample space. So the probability of sample space would be 1. Don’t think of $P(S)=0$ by assuming sample space is an empty set. Because we always convince ourself that there are always results of certain cases. If you think of there is no element in the sample space, there is actually no results of the case.
