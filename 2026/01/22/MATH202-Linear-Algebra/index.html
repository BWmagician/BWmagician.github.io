Inversion

The inversion in matrix multiplication is defined to be

Only one inversion exists

We say $A$ has two inversions, they are $B$ and $C$ and $B$ is different from $C$.

In the end we find $B$ equals $C$ which means $A$ only can’t have multiple inversion.

Why this definition?

You might feel confused why can’t we only have half of the definition,

Assume $A$ is a square matrix, $m$ by $m$, and is invertible (This means $A^{-1}$ exists only on the right side). You will find in subspaces of matrix why only square matrix can have inversions on its both sides (both $AA^{-1}$ and $A^{-1}A=I$).
